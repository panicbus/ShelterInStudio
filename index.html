Victoria Mara Heilweil

How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
I have my printer in my home studio/office, so am able to continue to work while stuck at home. I am spending even more time than usual in front of a screen, and am sharing my space with both my husband and daughter. I use my daily walks as a time to get away from screens and be by myself, in addition to getting some exercise, so it seemed a perfect time to make art too.
Have you created new work during this period?
Yes. I definitely felt that I needed to make work, and wanted to have a daily ritual. I decided at the start of shelter in place to photograph during my daily walks. My art is about noticing things, and about beauty, which I brought to this current project. Each day I photograph what catches my eye as beautiful and then I post it to Instagram the following morning. I decided early on to make this project a fundraiser for five organizations responding to COVID-19: SF-Marin Food Bank, Meals on Wheels, ACLU, Tipping Point and Safety Net Fund for Bay Area Artists. I feel fortunate that both my husband and I still have our jobs and income, and I wanted to do something to give back to the larger community. For a $50 donation of more, donors can get an 8.5x11" print from this series.
Has your work changed in any way during this period?
I feel that it has freed me up, and brought me back to my pure love of photography. Also, I was already thinking in terms of creating work that was site specific, and this project by it’s nature is limited to my neighborhood - about a 20 block radius. I also am loving having a daily practice - that is something I want to keep even after the shelter in place order is lifted. I’m also enjoying the challenges of finding new images each day. It’s very spontaneous and immediate.
What have some of your challenges been?
Time (as always). I’m still teaching, and moving my classes online was more time consuming in some ways than teaching in person.
What have some of your successes been?
I have found this to be a generative time for me. I have made lots of photographs, and so far have raised over $1200 with my fundraiser. I feel like the art world is being more inclusive and open, which is exciting. These photos have been in an online exhibition with Borderline Arts Collective, included in an online publication - Quaranzine - and in an online exhibition called Lazy Susan 2 with Blue Arrangements. I was also inspired by many other photographers (and artists) that I know who were making new work as a result of the shelter in place order. In some cases they needed to change their way of making work, in others they changed their subject matter. I saw so much great art on my Instagram feed, that as a curator I felt it would make an interesting exhibition. SF Camerawork features lens-based projects I selected on their Instagram and website in a series called Quarantine Picture Show, and I will be moderating two dates of artists interviews from this series for them in the upcoming weeks.

Justin Charles Hoover

How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
It has allowed me to open up in new ways. As life goes on it gets more complex and things get into ruts. So this has been a very interesting forced path out of those ruts. . Of course it's scary and concerning and confusing, but at the same time I"ve had the time to do a whole new thing that I've never done before.
Have you created new work during this period?
Yes, I created one event and one new land work. Specifically, I've envisioned a staircase as a performative sculpture. I've seen it in my mind for years and even did some site visits to potential spaces over the years, but nothing ever came of it. Now I'm carving this out of the hillside behind my house and it feels so right. I have also made a new social practice project - a 24-hour dance marathon as a community relief event and a fundraiser, as well as a design for a new food/stories project in my War Gastronomy series.
Has your work changed in any way during this period?
This earthwork / land artwork is a whole new direction for me.
What have some of your challenges been?
Learning curve of moving earth and driving rebar.
What have some of your successes been?
This has been the most fruitful time for me any my family. With two young children (under 5) in the house, I've seen how much they love and appreciate me around. I bring them joy and pleasure and sustenance. My presence helps them in so many ways I never expected it would. My son has become noticeably gentler and happier and more efflusive with his affection. We are all having an unexpected great time.

Erin McCluskey Wheeler

artist bio
How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
I am fortunate in that my art studio is at my home - the biggest change is that now I am never alone in my house. Pre quarantine, I had arranged my schedule with days where I taught art and days where I made art. And now my schedule is weird and irregular and very much dependent on my daughter and partner being home with me all the time. I spend a lot of time homeschooling and making meals and clearing up.
Have you created new work during this period?

It took me about 3 - 4 weeks into the quarantine to figure out how to make art and if I even wanted to make art. But now I am making small and quick art.

I had two large commissions that came in at the end of January; I finished one in February but I had to ask for an extension from the buyer for the second. I’m finally getting going on it.

Has your work changed in any way during this period?

My colors have shifted and are intense and brighter than before. I keep using a deep yellow / orange color in my work.

In early 2020, I had joined forces with an artist in North Carolina on a cool social practice collage project called The Collage Stop (@TheCollageStop). Meredith had solicited paper scrap donations from two collage artists - myself and Holly Cahill in Chicago. We sent our scraps to Meredith who then led a community collage making project in North Carolina. Meredith did a brilliant job facilitating the workshop and reported that there was something really magical for participants to work with our art materials; a lot of cool conversations happened around this project and we were ready to do more. I had the second event scheduled for Richmond in April. Meredith had sent me two bags of scraps just as shelter in place orders were being established. One bag was from Holly (an artist I’ve never met) and one bag was from Meredith (an artist I’ve met once). Opening these bags up and finding the marks, colors, and gestures left by other artists on these papers was the biggest gift. I also had received a set of papers from another artist I’ve never met in Brooklyn, who had sent me scraps from her sketchbooks. And my students had given me scraps they were done with after our last class together in March. I’ve been making a bunch of new work using these gifted and collected papers. I’m using all these bits and pieces from other artists during this time and it feels really good - like a conversation in color, and from a distance.

What have some of your challenges been?
I’ve always been good at reinvention and seeing new possibilities. But this is a lot of reinvention. Somedays I am just exhausted. I am not in the studio as much as I used to be. I wonder when we will ever be able to look at art in person, in groups, again. Those first few days and weeks there was so much loss. My spring and summer exhibitions were cancelled. The gallery that represents me closed its doors. I lost all my art teaching jobs and most of my income. Slowly and bit by bit, some teaching opportunities have returned and I’m offering online classes, meeting with students one on one, and making video lessons for kids. It’s not the same at all. I miss teaching and my students and making art in community. I worry about the art centers and galleries and who will be able to survive this.
What have some of your successes been?
I am really excited about the super bright art I’m making. When I do have time to get into my studio (literally just sitting down in the room at the end of the house by the back door) I can get something started and then keeping puttering and working on it slowly for days. I like having a piece in progress. I also started making and selling collage kits on my website. I put together a bunch of color sorted painted and found papers, a ready to go art making session. I’ve mailed them all over the country and sold 30 kits in 2 weeks. I really hope the folks buying these kits feel like they are part of an art making conversation with me.

Michael Zheng

artist bio
How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
All-in-all it's been positive (sorry no pun intended.). The shelter-in-place has made it easier for me to focus on making art.
Have you created new work during this period?

Yes. In fact, I have been super productive and have developed the drawings into a new body of work.

This period coincides with a flow situation that I have been in since the beginning of this year, when I started doing line drawings. It was triggered by a performance that I did in Victoria, British Columbia at the end of last year, for which I did a drawing as meditation on a wall.

Since I came home, I have been doing similar drawings on paper. The quarantine took place soon after that and it kept the distractions away and I have been burying myself in the studio doing these drawings. Since then I have developed it into a series of work based on a simple idea of "Draw A Line And Follow It". I'm still in the midst of that flow, which is really great.

Has your work changed in any way during this period?
Yes drastically. Prior to this, I had been doing performance predominantly. Now I'm fully occupied doing line drawings.
What have some of your challenges been?

The main challenge has been the inability to show works due to the Coronavirus. Several shows have been canceled, including a wall drawing performance in Oregon. On the other hand, because the show was canceled, I decided to do the wall drawing in my own studio and I like what happened.

The other difficulty is that I haven't been able to see friends. The lack of human contact has been hard.

What have some of your successes been?
The undistracted large chunk of time has allowed me not to rush in my work. I have been able to take time to really simmer the works. The rigor and creativity seem to be unusually high. Small breakthroughs happen more often. In fact, after I've experimented with the various drawings on paper, now I'm doing it again on large walls, but with nuances and a new dimension resulted from the experiments on paper. I'm very happy about that.

Douglas Kleinsmith

How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?

Initially, the shutdown negatively effected my practice. The disruption of my daily habits lead to a decrease in motivation and drive, and I overestimated how much time I had in each day. Much of my time was spent doing other things and with the amount of extra time I had, I felt as if I had plenty of time to paint, so I didn’t work in the present. After about a month, this passed and I actively decided to dedicate a part of every day to painting.

For several years I have been working through a major transition in my work, especially regarding my emotional relationship with my process, and initially, things didn’t feel any different. I was frustrated and dissatisfied with what I was making. However the shutdown and all of this extra time I had catalyzed an awareness of a thought pattern that was influencing my every move when I was painting: I was always in a hurry. I always needed to be done, not doing, I was always behind. Others were achieving success and I had a lot of catching up to do. I was never fully engaged with my work; my head was always in the future and needed every painting to be done. I needed success. But now with all this extra time, no day job to go to, no deadlines for shows or applications... Why was I still hurrying? What's the rush? It was quite visceral when I had this "aha" moment, I literally had this cathartic wave of euphoria wash over my body and it made me laugh with relief. I realized how these thoughts were always running in the background, and how they had a direct impact on my emotional state while I was creating. I painted for a few hours that night and it felt quite effortless and enjoyable. It helped me get back in touch with the necessary presence, detachment, and playfulness that’s essential for creativity.

I wish I could say that my work has forever changed because of that one moment, but I still find myself going back to my old habits, feeling anxious and rushed. But thankfully I now have an awareness of my state of mind and am actively trying to maintain a healthier relationship with creating.

Have you created new work during this period?
Yes, I’ve produced about four or five new paintings, and have progressed on many others that were in the works before the lockdown.
Has your work changed in any way during this period?
It has definitely become looser, more relaxed. My work is abstract, utilizing a lot of gestural paint application and intuitive color choices, and Ive noticed myself being less methodical and uptight about the outcome. I am more engaged with the process and more accepting of failure, which has lead to dramatically different results.
What have some of your challenges been?
My biggest challenge has been letting go of the outcome. My old mental habits still need every painting, every mark, to be right. Every piece needs to be better than everything I've done before... Which is just another aspect of the destructive emotional habits and egoic desires that have ultimately been hindering my progress. So while it's not related directly to the lockdown, it's what I've been wrestling with. Im fortunate enough to have financial stability, a home studio, and ample supplies to create, so my challenges have been mostly internal.
What have some of your successes been?
My work has taken a turn because of my new and developing awareness, which has definitely been my greatest success.

Steve Briscoe

How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?

I disagree with the word "stuck". It has been great to have the distractions of tasks outside the home paused and I have felt pretty content staying in. While the stay at home order has been in place since March, I have been working in the studio steadily since I left my last job. I have been underemployed or self-employed for the past year and a half, so there has not been the Zoom meetings and other trappings of trying to work from home.

The adjustment has been in finding a rhythm and balance to the household chores and studio work time and family time. Sometimes that studio time is adjacent to the workout time and breakfast time and does not require a costume change! Cooking new things has become a more communal and creative act and provides the novelty that going out used to, and it feels like learning new things is a productive way to spend time.

While I have been fine with being at home, there is a visual richness that I am missing. I will be glad to get back to some regular day trips or even shopping that provides some novelty and inspiration. I am a sculptor mostly and depend on cast off things and materials for my work. I am steadily working off my back stock of wood, even making projects sized to fit the materials I have. I can mail order some hardware and tools, but it is a deprivation not to just hop out to the hardware store to look at options. Much like the grocery shopping, it is better done in person.

Have you created new work during this period?

I was preparing for a couple of show opportunities this year, so I had a lot going on in the studio. I finished several pieces that were in progress at the time of the shutdown and began others that are in the same vein. At first, it seemed like a temporary slowdown and we would be back up and running in a few months. As the time has past and it appears more and more like our world has changed for the foreseeable future, I will need to rethink the necessity of making every piece I think of. I make things because I must, but not being able to share them in a real space with others would take some of the fun out of it.

Oddly, I had been writing a short story, and this whole plot twist of a pandemic is either in it or not. There will be no avoiding it except by dating the content. I need to decide whether to include this or ignore it and proceed as planned.

Has your work changed in any way during this period?

Too soon to tell. It took me more than a year to absorb 9/11 and even then, what I produced was fairly oblique to the tragedy; more of a response to the response.

I am resisting virus imagery as too facile and obvious, though I have used DNA strands and other "cell" type things in my earlier paintings. But eventually there will be some shorthand that will appear to represent the whole catastrophe.

What I have found is that I am working at a different pace. Because there is no real deadline or known easing of restrictions date, I am not pressured to finish which is sometimes hard for me. There are lots of starts and pauses while things get mulled over and decided before they are completed. I am also trying some different media, because I am feeling a bit crowded by sculpture. I am working on some photography projects and printmaking ideas. The less one makes, the more room there is for conceptual pieces that may exist only on social media. If we are all broadcasting from our bubbles, it could be a new theatrical renaissance in the artworld.

What have some of your challenges been?

I feel exceedingly lucky to be in a position where I have a house and studio, a partner that I love. I have income and I don’t have to telecommute to keep afloat. I am semi-retired with some health challenges but overall, I am not suffering as greatly as some.

The main challenge has been keeping motivated and a positive attitude while the world rages around outside. I have to make stuff in the studio to keep it at bay. People are dying at an alarming rate and there is nothing I can do but stay at home and keep healthy? It seems an inadequate response. However, I have to believe that in the act of creation there is a replacement or replenishment of the spirits that we lose to the virus, conflict or old age. If we survive, and that remains in doubt, we have to live, love and create for the ones who did not fulfil their life’s work.

What have some of your successes been?

Before the shutdown, I had been examining older work and trying to place it in collections. With the extra time at home, I have begun cataloging more of it and sending out emails with images in the hopes that someone will take an interest. I have several bodies of work that never really got an airing in public and I am hoping to produce a book of some of my photography.

I am not sure if that is a success but reflection on the past is helpful in charting a forward direction. I also have uncovered sketchbooks with some dead ends that I would like to revisit so perhaps there is more change coming to my work. It certainly seems like a great time to take risks and go down a few rabbit holes to see what develops.

Perhaps the success is stopping to think about where we are now and what kind of a world are we stepping into from now on. We are appreciating the things we took for granted, like a crowded concert or art gallery or a dinner party with friends. The isolation we now seek as a safe refuge is becoming a constraint that we are not programmed for. We miss the interactions with other people, known and unknown, who are not wearing masks and who do us the human kindness of acknowledging us.

Lexa Walsh

artist bio
How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
Interestingly, it has been positive on many levels. I've had a ton of time to make art, and plenty of inspiration, though not in the ways I thought I would be productive. Plus, I have an affordable studio, which I share only with my partner, so we can easily and safely go there, together or separately. On the other hand my work schedule in "normal" times rotates a month on, a month off, so it doesn't feel as weird as it might for others.
Have you created new work during this period?
I've created several bodies of new work, many which I can't really finish because they are in ceramics. I've also made some linocuts, some sewn works, and I've brought some old works out for a new look. I have come up with lots of project ideas, and am generally inspired.
Has your work changed in any way during this period?
Well, as described above, I'm making a lot of objects for a change. Many of the works have an activist impulse, which is weird for me- not being an activist, but to make objects that are clearly such. I'm crafting things with my hands- that has not been important to me for many years. I'm working in a studio practice way, opposed to a project-based one. I'm allowing immediate impulses to guide me, and feeling very free to experiment. If not now, when?
What have some of your challenges been?
I have little inspiration to make online socially engaged projects. My strength in that regard is being a people person. My challenge with Social Practice has always been with how much time is spent on the computer- so I have not found solace knowing that I might have to go deeper into that hole. I want to be present in real life, with whatever project I do followed by a real hug... . I also have several projects that require online work and that would be easy and fortuitous to do now, but again I am finding it hard to work on the computer too much with the addition of all the zoom meetings. Also, I've been sleeping like crap so my regular schedule and energy levels are irregular, and thus I might not be using my time as efficiently as I am used to. All of my paid work has been cancelled through at least October- that's scary! Oh, and we got kittens. They are distracting.
What have some of your successes been?
I've been very productive, even if I'm not sure where the work belongs in my overall practice. I've been meeting with another artist every week to discuss professional development work. I feel liberated. I've developed relationships with other artists that I would normally not have time for. My family has been meeting every week, too, and that, along with my senses being open, has led to several ideas. I'm lucky that I qualified for some unemployment and got an emergency grant so I feel so much gratitude that I am not completely screwed financially. And we got kittens. They are adorable.

Eliza Gregory

How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?

I’m the mother of an eight-year-old and a one-year-old. I was the primary caregiver for both girls prior to quarantine,and worked part time for Sacramento State as a photography professor thanks to a collage of childcare from three different providers. My eight-year-old is in second grade at our local school. Now I am trying to homeschool my second grader and provide all the care for my baby, except for one day a week when we have retained one of our babysitters for a few hours. He lives alone and is a very responsible quarantin-er, and he’s working on his PhD, so he’s able to control how many people he sees. We’re essentially quarantining together with him. So I have almost no time where I can reliably work. The six hours a week I get on Fridays go toward a little exercise, some more focused time with my older daughter—because she’s really been struggling with all the changes in her life, and the loss of school and her teachers and her friends—and sometimes some house projects that I can’t do with the baby, grocery shopping, or a phone call to a friend, or a little bit of time drawing, painting and taking classes in watercolor and drawing.

I’m a social practice artist, and a photographer, so I’m not usually a mark maker. Even before quarantine, I had been feeling a strong urge to make work in a new or different way than I have in the past ten years or so. I came to the end of a series of projects that were all very similar, and it was a really positive, exciting end, and I wanted to take time after that to think about how I would embark on my next longer-term project, and how to really make sure the micro-level of making was different in it, as well as the theme and the overall structure. So now that I can’t put serious chunks of time into anything professional, it has felt great to try to learn some new skills by taking online classes—informally, through CreativeBug—and to go back to the kinds of playful making I used to do as a kid and a young adult. Basically, I’m treating art as a hobby at the moment, instead of as a profession, and that’s really fun and both novel and comforting. Although it’s very temporary—very much tied to this time where my baby can’t be left alone and I’m constantly on call for her. It’s also the sort of fallow period between big projects, where you start to gather yourself for the next one, both intellectually and energetically.

I’ve read lots of articles where parents describe their heroic patterns of working at night after their children are asleep... that’s just not a reality for me. I am not a night person—I get to about 7 pm after a full day of interacting with my kids and I can barely make a sentence anymore, let alone do complex intellectual, emotional or physical work. I always see art as the highest possible form of intellectual pursuit—not that it’s higher than other things, but that it’s equal to other things that we would think of conventionally as the height of complexity and skill. That doesn’t mean that every practitioner reaches the highest expression of the form, but I always want to be working toward that as a goal. So that’s not something I want to tackle while brain-dead. And I feel pretty silly noodling around with watercolor and when I have no real skills in that department, and definitely no complexity or subtlety. But I also believe in finding ways to make yourself happy, following intuition, and trying to preserve mental health as both a person in quarantine and as a parent, so I’m trying to stifle the judgement and just allow myself to have fun. Learning this kind of skill and doing small works on paper are also things I can be interrupted in and still come back to without losing my place, so to speak. So they are really fitting in with my current limitations. And I can these things with my older daughter, which feels great and helps me support her learning.

I miss working a lot. I definitely worry about my professional momentum, and have moments where I wonder what’s going to emerge from this time. I am certainly not immune to existential professional doubt. (And fear and anxiety in general.) But those things pale in comparison to my awareness of the extreme privilege I am speaking from. I have had—and continue to have—so many choices in both my personal and professional life, and so much support from my family. Nothing about my concerns compares to the job insecurity, food insecurity, home insecurity, and health insecurity that most people are dealing with in one way or another right now. My biggest day to day worry is keeping my mental health good, so that I can be a good mom and support my girls appropriately as we all get through this. I think everyone is having to work hard to find mental and emotional and physical health right now—some people amidst extreme adversity. And any one of us could be on the brink of seeing our lives turned upside down, or even end abruptly.

Have you created new work during this period?

I have an ongoing practice of photographing my children in nature, and during certain moments of their childhoods that say something about my parenting, their environment, or their personalities. That’s something I developed when I was first home with my older daughter, when she was a baby. So that’s seamlessly woven into my daily life and I am always making new work to a certain extent through that modality. Though it has a very limited scope and audience—I just post those pictures to a private Instagram account.

I’m also constantly researching. I’m thinking about a new project now, so I’m looking at things interest me that could tie into it, and learning about other artists, ideas and resources that could help me once I really begin the work.

I’ve also been making these small works on paper, mostly in the form of cards I send to friends and family. I’ve toyed with the idea of making them printable, so that I could release them to a wider audience, but I haven’t moved ahead with that yet.

Has your work changed in any way during this period?
I have found myself trying to adapt some of the intellectual ways that I approach my work to my parenting more overtly recently. I always think about parenting creatively, and try to bring some of the same intentionality to my life that I bring to my work, but I think now that I am kind of trapped in my personal life without ANY professional life, I’m trying to do that even more, and help myself be even more aware of it, so that I can find more joy in what I am doing. Every few weeks I feel myself hit a wall and I try to recommit to enjoying my daughters and this kind of relentless time with them. Interestingly, it’s really not that different from what I wanted for myself anyway, while my baby is a baby (I love babies!) but removing just those few extra hours of time where I could work each week has affected my happiness a lot. I was also teaching last term, and I’m not this term, and that’s very different. I love the intellectual and social gymnastics of teaching, and I miss my students and my colleagues. Though I don’t know how I’d be managing to continue teaching remotely while caring for a maniacal baby, so I think I lucked out there overall.
What have some of your challenges been?
  • Zero professional relevance outside the home.
  • A worry that I am not helping my community enough at this difficult time.
  • Maintaining energy and creativity amidst the demands of my domestic work.
  • Not being able to work at all as a professional.
What have some of your successes been?
Learning, and wanting to learn. Making small works, even if they are silly and decorative, with zero depth, nuance or skill. ;) Cooking beautiful meals for my family. Helping my daughters learn at home (and only at home.) Feeling in love with my partner, and appreciating all that he does for our family. Feeling good about myself as a mom, neighbor, colleague, friend, daughter, sister and wife.

Jeremiah Jenkins

How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
The hardest impact has been on my responsibilities outside of my art practice. With two young children, an in person ceramics teaching job, and online art history classes it has been difficult to transition to being at home full time. I also have not had access to my usual studio or certain materials. I had a show planned for October, but the gallery has dropped off the map and are not answering my emails and calls. I am however still working on things.
Have you created new work during this period?
Since the limitations have hindered what I was planning on doing, I have taken to a deep experimental process. I have built a wood kiln in my backyard and am currently exploring what that will lead to. I have no idea if it will all come together into something that fits with my usual body of work, but I really don't care at the moment.
Has your work changed in any way during this period?
I've been interested in the same types of things for sometime now, but I am feeling more of a draw towards these ideas. I'm obsessed with human history, ingenuity, and accessing the past to make sense of the future. I think this mode of thinking has served me well in the past and is helping me get through the present.
What have some of your challenges been?
My challenges have been mostly domestic. Making sure one kid is engaging with school work while the other is also growing and learning. Breaking up fights over who gets what cup, toy, or whatever. Getting the kids to not go into the apartment downstairs where my mother in law who doesn't believe in the pandemic lives. Going to the grocery store all suited up and then stripping down, showering, then wiping each item with alcohol. Dealing with the overwhelming stress in my daily thoughts, reactions, and in my body where it manifests into intense muscular pain. All the while trying to come up with some idea that will make people think and feel, plus finding the time to make it.
What have some of your successes been?
When I was 14 I grew 6 inches over the summer. I had severe "growing pains" and cramps in my legs, but by September I was as taller and stronger. I feel like that now. I hear the divine mysterious voice that tells artists what to do and it's quite loud. I feel myself becoming more something, I'm just not sure what it is. If I had more time and/or money I would be producing a shit ton of work, but that's always the case. I am however working with what I've got, exploring, and discovering.

artist bio
How has being stuck at home affected your art practice?
Although I hesitate to go to my remote art studio in San Pablo, I stay at home in Albany and work on my home studio. I have more time to concentrate on my art since I save commute time for the studio and places to teach classes.
Have you created new work during this period?
Yes, I have created many of small pieces.
Has your work changed in any way during this period?

I paint more small pieces rather than large ones.

I practice more calligraphy than before. Almost everyday I practice heart sutra or zen words.

I creating my art hoping we will regain our healthy world as soon as possible.

What have some of your challenges been?
Online classes might be good for people can take my classes from anywhere. However, I really miss in person class where I can share my qui/inner power to create a fine brush painting that is hard to do online.

Has your work changed in any way during this period?
Dramatically. I make sculpture and performance and social practice, all of which rely on public spaces, access to parks and the outdoors, and other people. Place and environment are some of my biggest inspirations—when the shelter in place order was announced, I was just getting into rigging my camera with an underwater case so I could study mussels growing in the Alameda estuary. Now I'm primarily studying bivalves through reading. Did you know the city of Warsaw has eight living clams rigged with switches that tell the city when the water supply is contaminated? They look, structurally speaking, exactly like my sculptures. I'm not sure if I would have come across these little guys had I not been spending hours and hours on the internet and on the phone with my art friends, one of whom sent me a link to the documentary about the clams ("Fat Kathy" just debuted yesterday).
What have some of your challenges been?
The most interesting thing that has happened is in my long term project, which is to try to discover if a plant that moves with sunlight (called "nyctinastic" movement) could be consulted to set camera exposure. I've constructed five of these biohacker lightmeters and have been growing different nyctinastic plants in each since December. I had pretty much abandoned four, but the fifth, mimosa pudica, though growing the slowest was making the most reliable movement between day and night. I set up a timelapse video to capture its movement. Ten hours into the timelapse my cat devoured the plant whole. All that was left was a little twiggy stem. I had set out to try to establish an interspecies relationship—I would provide the plant with ideal growing conditions, and it would tell me about the sun. I didn't count on a third species and her desires and needs.
What have some of your successes been?
I'm really excited about the amount of research I've been able to do and how that will inform what I make when I have more space and my shop at my disposal. I went down a rabbit hole looking at the history of mimosa pudica in scientific literature. It turns out to have inspired the first description of circadian rhythm, written in France by Jacques de Mairan, and continues to be a plant that is used for research on plant memory and communication. I discovered through de Mairan that a Swedish botanist, Carl Linnaeus, hypothesized a clock that relies on flowers that open at different hours of the morning. When you find that a rope you’ve been pulling is attached to a whole system that was previously unseen is so rewarding.

Stay In Studio Project

Due to Covid-19 the world has been put on pause. We are all sheltered in place and responding to an ever evolving situation. In this climate of collective anxiety and uncertainty I have been trying to focus on keeping an active studio practice and developing a new body of work. It has been stressful and nerve-wracking at times but also intensely enlightening and gratifying. When will I ever again have endless hours to develop new concepts, go down new rabbit holes and take risks in my work I might not normally take.

As a Social Practice artist I am curious how other artists are coping with this situation and have created a project called "Stay In Studio". My goal is to reach out to 100 California Artists and ask them the questions below. Responses can be as deep or brief as you like, its your choice.

These responses will live on a website I'm designing that will be updated as new participants come in. It is my hope that this content will turn into a published book and I am in the process of applying for several grants to help make this possible.

When these terrible times pass we can look back at a collective document of how hard working, resilient and adaptive California Artists were in response to a really tough situation.
